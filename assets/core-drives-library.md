# Core Drives Library

This file is the reusable asset library for CD1-CD8. Each drive is expressed as a diagnostic and intervention unit, not as a list of tricks.

## CD1 — Epic Meaning And Calling

**Definition**: The actor believes the behavior serves something larger than the self, expresses a valued identity, or answers a "calling."

**Signals**

- The actor uses identity language: "people like us," "this matters," "I want to contribute."
- The behavior is sustained despite weak direct personal reward.
- Participants tolerate inconvenience because the mission feels credible.
- Community rituals, slogans, or shared symbols reinforce purpose.

**Underuse**

- The behavior feels like a chore, transaction, or isolated task.
- Participants cannot explain why the action matters beyond immediate utility.
- Advocacy is weak because the action lacks identity or story.

**Overuse / Misuse**

- Mission language sounds fake or self-serving.
- A brand/system claims purpose without sacrifice or proof.
- A fictional narrative is imposed where the audience wants seriousness.
- Meaning becomes moral pressure rather than empowerment.

**Interventions**

- Link the behavior to a real long-term identity, contribution, or shared future.
- Create a credible meaning narrative: meaning, vision, fable, or myth.
- Show sacrifice or tradeoff that proves sincerity.
- Reinforce meaning after repeated actions, not only at signup.
- Let participants see cumulative real-world or group impact.

**Risks**

- Cause-washing, manipulation, ideological pressure.
- Alienating users who do not share the mission.
- Using moral identity to excuse poor functionality.

**Diagnostic Questions**

- What larger purpose, identity, group, or future does this behavior serve?
- Is the mission credible to this audience, or will it feel insulting?
- What evidence or sacrifice proves the mission is real?
- Does the behavior produce visible impact, or only rhetoric?

**Representative Case Patterns**

- Wikipedia contributors protect shared knowledge.
- Patagonia builds credibility through environmental sacrifice.
- Ant Forest and Ocean Hero turn ordinary actions into visible environmental contribution.
- Waze frames driving data as fighting traffic together.

## CD2 — Development And Accomplishment

**Definition**: The actor is motivated by visible progress, growing competence, meaningful challenge, and completion.

**Signals**

- People ask, "How am I doing?" or "What is next?"
- Progress, status, certification, levels, or milestones matter.
- The behavior has a clear before/after state.
- Participants are energized by overcoming a challenge.

**Underuse**

- Effort feels invisible.
- The actor cannot tell whether they are improving.
- Tasks feel either too easy to matter or too hard to enter.
- Completion has no emotional reward.

**Overuse / Misuse**

- Badges or scores are awarded without real challenge.
- People optimize for points instead of quality or learning.
- Competition discourages low-status participants.
- Achievement metrics crowd out meaning or creativity.

**Interventions**

- Define meaningful milestones and completion states.
- Make progress visible and tied to real competence.
- Calibrate challenge to skill level.
- Celebrate completion with feedback that confirms capability.
- Use credentials/status only when they signal real achievement.

**Risks**

- Metric gaming.
- Anxiety and comparison pressure.
- Reducing rich activity to a scoreboard.

**Diagnostic Questions**

- What does progress look like from the participant's point of view?
- Is the challenge meaningful enough for achievement to matter?
- Is the actor improving skill or merely collecting symbols?
- What is the "win state" after the target behavior?

**Representative Case Patterns**

- LinkedIn profile completion makes progress visible.
- Certifications and mastery paths support professional motivation when credible.
- HTC/VIVE improved conversion by clarifying the next action and win state.

## CD3 — Empowerment Of Creativity And Feedback

**Definition**: The actor can make meaningful choices, experiment, express strategy or style, and receive feedback that lets them adjust.

**Signals**

- People enjoy trying different approaches.
- The activity remains interesting without constant new content.
- Participants ask for tools, sandboxes, customization, or feedback.
- The behavior includes autonomy and experimentation.

**Underuse**

- The process is rigid, scripted, and passive.
- People have no meaningful choices or ways to adapt.
- Feedback is delayed, opaque, or absent.
- The actor feels pushed rather than invited.

**Overuse / Misuse**

- Too many choices cause confusion.
- "Creativity" is cosmetic and does not affect outcomes.
- Experimentation lacks feedback, so learning stalls.
- Autonomy is offered before the actor understands the system.

**Interventions**

- Give constrained choices that matter.
- Provide rapid feedback after experiments.
- Let actors configure, remix, test, and improve.
- Design tools that turn passive observers into active contributors.
- Use prompts that invite agency rather than pressure compliance.

**Risks**

- Cognitive overload.
- Safety issues if experimentation affects health, finance, or public systems.
- Creative work being undermined by extrinsic rewards.

**Diagnostic Questions**

- Where can the actor make a meaningful choice?
- What feedback arrives quickly enough to teach the next move?
- Is autonomy real or cosmetic?
- Would adding a reward reduce creative quality?

**Representative Case Patterns**

- LEGO-like systems remain engaging because the mind generates new content.
- Autodesk's trial experience made product use itself part of the challenge.
- Strategy dashboards should enable design exploration, not just checklist completion.

## CD4 — Ownership And Possession

**Definition**: The actor feels they own, have built, personalized, accumulated, or are responsible for something.

**Signals**

- People return because their data, history, setup, or assets are there.
- Customization increases attachment.
- Participants protect or improve what they have built.
- The system learns preferences and becomes "mine."

**Underuse**

- Actions disappear after completion.
- The actor gains no asset, memory, profile, or accumulated capability.
- Repeated work feels disposable.
- The system does not recognize prior investment.

**Overuse / Misuse**

- Accumulated assets trap people through sunk cost.
- Users feel unable to leave without losing identity or history.
- Collection becomes hoarding, compulsion, or meaningless clutter.
- Ownership is used to justify lock-in.

**Interventions**

- Turn completed behavior into visible assets or capabilities.
- Personalize the experience based on real use.
- Let actors improve, protect, organize, or reuse what they build.
- Provide portability or graceful exit to avoid coercive lock-in.

**Risks**

- Sunk-cost prison.
- Data privacy and personalization creep.
- Loss anxiety.

**Diagnostic Questions**

- What does the actor own after acting?
- Does repeated behavior create a reusable asset or only a transient checkmark?
- Is personalization useful, transparent, and consentful?
- Can users leave without feeling punished?

**Representative Case Patterns**

- Waze and similar systems gain attachment through learned routes/preferences.
- Profiles, collections, and certificates can support ownership when meaningful.
- McDonald's Monopoly-style collections show both engagement power and collection risk.

## CD5 — Social Influence And Relatedness

**Definition**: The actor is motivated by belonging, recognition, reciprocity, mentorship, social proof, comparison, empathy, competition, or shared identity.

**Signals**

- People care what peers, mentors, customers, teammates, or community members think.
- Social proof or norm messages change behavior.
- Participants return for relationships, visibility, or recognition.
- Referrals happen when sharing strengthens identity or relationship.

**Underuse**

- The behavior is isolated.
- Participants cannot see peers or receive recognition.
- Trust is weak because no relatable others validate the behavior.
- Collaboration, mentorship, or reciprocity is missing.

**Overuse / Misuse**

- Social pressure becomes shame, coercion, harassment, or status anxiety.
- Leaderboards demotivate most participants.
- Norm messages accidentally normalize bad behavior.
- Referral systems turn friends into extraction targets.

**Interventions**

- Show positive, relevant, accurate social norms.
- Create recognition loops that do not humiliate low performers.
- Use peer support, mentorship, shared accountability, and reciprocity.
- Segment social proof: "people like you" must be credible.
- Design social treasure: participants can give value to others.

**Risks**

- Backfire from negative norm framing.
- Toxic comparison.
- Privacy violations.
- Social spam.

**Diagnostic Questions**

- Which peers matter to this actor?
- Is comparison helpful, or would recognition/support work better?
- Does the design make good behavior feel normal?
- Could the social mechanism pressure or expose vulnerable users?

**Representative Case Patterns**

- Opower-style relevant neighbor comparison.
- Arizona park warning backfire when bad behavior was framed as common.
- Pinduoduo group buying shows strong social loops and irritation risk.
- Amazon recommendations use "people like you" proof.

## CD6 — Scarcity And Impatience

**Definition**: The actor wants something more because it is limited, unavailable, exclusive, gated, difficult, or time-bound.

**Signals**

- People return for a window, opening, appointment, waitlist, unlock, or limited slot.
- Desire increases when access is constrained.
- Gated content or qualification creates anticipation.
- Participants ask, "How do I unlock this?"

**Underuse**

- Everything is always available and undifferentiated.
- There is no sense of timing, sequence, or earned access.
- High-value moments feel flat because they are not framed as special.

**Overuse / Misuse**

- Fake scarcity destroys trust.
- Countdown timers create anxiety without real value.
- Gating blocks needed access or harms equity.
- Scarcity becomes dark-pattern pressure.

**Interventions**

- Use honest constraints: cohorts, capacity, sequence, qualification, skill gate.
- Show clear unlock paths.
- Pair scarcity with CD2 challenge and CD3 agency.
- Use scarcity to focus attention, not to deceive.

**Risks**

- FOMO manipulation.
- Exclusion and inequity.
- Long-term distrust if scarcity is artificial.

**Diagnostic Questions**

- Is the scarcity real, useful, and transparent?
- Does the actor know how to unlock access?
- Does the window improve timing, focus, or community, or just pressure?
- What white-hat alternative could create urgency?

**Representative Case Patterns**

- Early Facebook school-by-school rollout.
- Woot daily deals.
- Cohort programs and certification gates when tied to real constraints.

## CD7 — Unpredictability And Curiosity

**Definition**: The actor is motivated by uncertainty, surprise, mystery, unresolved information, variable outcomes, or discovery.

**Signals**

- People keep checking because they want to know what happens next.
- Mystery or reveal sequences hold attention.
- Variable feedback makes repeated action more compelling.
- Discovery creates excitement even without large rewards.

**Underuse**

- The experience is too predictable and stale.
- There is no discovery, novelty, or open loop.
- Participants know exactly what will happen every time and disengage.

**Overuse / Misuse**

- Variable rewards create compulsive checking or gambling-like behavior.
- Surprise becomes distraction or noise.
- Mystery hides important terms or manipulates consent.
- People lose trust if outcomes feel rigged.

**Interventions**

- Add ethical mystery, previews, reveal moments, rotating prompts, or surprise feedback.
- Use curiosity to teach, explore, or invite attention.
- Keep uncertainty bounded and non-exploitative.
- Avoid variable rewards for vulnerable contexts.

**Risks**

- Addiction loops.
- Attention hijacking.
- Anxiety and compulsive checking.

**Diagnostic Questions**

- What open question makes the actor want to continue?
- Is the uncertainty entertaining, educational, or exploitative?
- Can the actor opt out or predict important consequences?
- Does curiosity serve the target behavior or distract from it?

**Representative Case Patterns**

- Blendtec's "what will happen?" content pattern.
- Mystery boxes and Easter eggs.
- Duolingo-style variety can sustain return, but streak/randomness risks need review.

## CD8 — Loss And Avoidance

**Definition**: The actor acts to avoid losing status, progress, money, access, identity, time, opportunity, relationships, or prior effort.

**Signals**

- People say they "have to" act or cannot stop.
- Streaks, sunk cost, expiration, FOMO, or penalties drive behavior.
- Avoiding regret or negative consequence is more salient than positive desire.
- Existing investment makes exit difficult.

**Underuse**

- There is no consequence for missing important windows.
- Actors underestimate real risks of inaction.
- Necessary safety, compliance, or health actions lack urgency.

**Overuse / Misuse**

- People feel trapped, anxious, or controlled.
- Streaks punish recovery and create shame.
- Sunk cost keeps people in harmful loops.
- Loss framing replaces meaningful motivation.

**Interventions**

- Use loss framing only for real consequences.
- Prefer reversible reminders, safety nets, and grace periods.
- Convert fear into agency: show what can still be protected.
- Follow black-hat activation with white-hat reassurance and control.

**Risks**

- Learned helplessness.
- Fatigue and churn after escape.
- Manipulative lock-in.
- Harm to mental health.

**Diagnostic Questions**

- What exactly would the actor lose by not acting?
- Is the loss real, proportionate, and transparent?
- Does the design preserve agency and recovery?
- What white-hat support should follow the urgent action?

**Representative Case Patterns**

- Forest App uses loss avoidance for focus, but must avoid shame.
- Penny auctions show sunk-cost escalation and extraction.
- Compliance and safety workflows may require CD8, but only with clear ethical limits.
